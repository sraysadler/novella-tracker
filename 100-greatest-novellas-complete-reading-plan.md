# The 100 Greatest Novellas: Complete Reading Plan

---

> **How to use this guide:** Start with the Quick Wins Path (Phase 1–4), which builds your reading confidence with 18 page-turners. Then move through the 10 Theme Clusters in order, or jump to whichever theme calls to you. Within each cluster, books are sequenced from most accessible to most challenging.

> **Key:**
> ✅ = Already read (covered earlier in the Quick Wins path)
> 📖 = Currently reading
> 🎬 = Notable film adaptation exists (details in notes)

---

# Part One: The Quick Wins Path

*The idea: start with novellas where the story does most of the work. Build momentum by finishing books. Build confidence by finishing great books.*

---

## Phase 1 — "I can't believe I just read four classics in a month."
*Plain language, gripping stories, all under 130 pages.*

| Order | Rank | Title | Author | Year | ~Pages | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 📖 | #26 | **Train Dreams** | Denis Johnson | 2002 | 116 | Your starting point. A day laborer's life across the American West — love, loss, and ghosts. Pulitzer finalist. |
| 1 | #3 | **Of Mice and Men** | John Steinbeck | 1937 | 107 | 🎬 *1992 film with Gary Sinise & John Malkovich.* Same Depression-era world as Train Dreams. Short sentences, devastating ending. You'll finish it in an evening. |
| 2 | #1 | **Animal Farm** | George Orwell | 1945 | 95 | 🎬 *1954 animated film; 1999 TV movie.* Reads like a fable — talking animals, simple language. The political meaning lands without effort. The #1 novella on the master list. |
| 3 | #7 | **A Christmas Carol** | Charles Dickens | 1843 | 80 | 🎬 *Dozens of versions: 1951 Alastair Sim (the classic), 1992 Muppet Christmas Carol, 2009 Jim Carrey.* You already know the story, which helps. Dickens is wordier than Steinbeck but the plot flies. |
| 4 | #9 | **The Strange Case of Dr. Jekyll and Mr. Hyde** | Robert Louis Stevenson | 1886 | 70 | 🎬 *1931 film (Fredric March, Oscar-winning); many others.* Essentially a mystery-thriller. Only 70 pages. Suspenseful even if you know the twist. |

## Phase 3 — "Now I want something with a little more weight."
*Still driven by strong narratives, but the writing itself starts doing more. You'll slow down to re-read a sentence — not because it's confusing, but because it's good.*

| Order | Rank | Title | Author | Year | ~Pages | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 10 | #15 | **Chronicle of a Death Foretold** | Gabriel García Márquez | 1981 | 120 | 🎬 *1987 Italian/Colombian film.* Everyone knows Santiago Nasar will be murdered. No one stops it. García Márquez tells it out of order, like a detective, and it's weirdly addictive. |
| 11 | #6 | **The Old Man and the Sea** | Ernest Hemingway | 1952 | 127 | 🎬 *1958 film (Spencer Tracy).* An old fisherman hooks a giant marlin. That's the whole plot. Hemingway's simplest prose; the emotion sneaks up on you. Won the Pulitzer; helped win him the Nobel. |
| 12 | #21 | **Chess Story** | Stefan Zweig | 1942 | 85 | 🎬 *The Royal Game (2021, German film).* Two strangers play chess on an ocean liner — one learned the game under Gestapo torture. A psychological thriller disguised as a board-game story. Zweig's final work before his suicide. |
| 13 | #2 | **The Stranger** | Albert Camus | 1942 | 123 | 🎬 *The Stranger (1967, Visconti, with Marcello Mastroianni).* A man kills someone almost by accident and feels nothing. Sounds intimidating ("existentialism!") but reads like a crime novel with a strange narrator. Short, blunt sentences. Far easier than its reputation. |
| 14 | #11 | **Breakfast at Tiffany's** | Truman Capote | 1958 | 100 | 🎬 *Breakfast at Tiffany's (1961, Audrey Hepburn).* Nothing like the movie. Capote's Holly Golightly is wilder, sadder, and more complicated. A story about loneliness in gorgeous but accessible prose. |

## Phase 4 — "I'm ready for Kafka."
*The experience is less about "what happens" and more about how the writing makes you feel.*

| Order | Rank | Title | Author | Year | ~Pages | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 15 | #5 | **The Metamorphosis** | Franz Kafka | 1915 | 55 | A man wakes up as a giant insect. That's the first sentence. The rest is about his family's reaction. Disturbing, darkly funny, and only 55 pages. You'll think about it for weeks. |
| 16 | #20 | **The Pearl** | John Steinbeck | 1947 | 90 | 🎬 *The Pearl (1947, Mexican film co-written by Steinbeck).* A poor diver finds an enormous pearl and it destroys everything he loves. Steinbeck again, so you'll already trust the voice. More parable than realistic story. |
| 17 | #4 | **Heart of Darkness** | Joseph Conrad | 1899 | 80 | 🎬 *Apocalypse Now (1979, Coppola) — loosely adapted. One of the greatest films ever made. Watch it first if you want.* Marlow's journey up the Congo River. The first genuinely challenging prose on this path. Conrad's sentences are long and layered, but you're never more than 80 pages from the end. |
| 18 | #29 | **Bonjour Tristesse** | Françoise Sagan | 1954 | 130 | 🎬 *Bonjour Tristesse (1958, Otto Preminger, with Jean Seberg).* A teenager deliberately sabotages her father's relationship on the French Riviera. Written by an actual eighteen-year-old. Sharp, cool, and surprisingly modern. |

**Quick Wins complete: 18 books read.** You've knocked out seven of the top 10 and built the reading muscles for anything on this list.

# Part Two: Theme Clusters

*Pick the cluster that matches your mood, or read them in order. Books you already read during the Quick Wins path are marked ✅ and included in their thematic home so you can see how they connect to the new reads.*

---

## Cluster 1: The Wild & the Elements
*Humans against landscape, weather, animals, and their own limits. Spare prose. Physical stakes. These novellas make you feel cold, tired, and alive.*

| Order | Rank | Title | Author | Year | ~Pages | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 📖 | #26 | **Train Dreams** | Denis Johnson | 2002 | 116 | Currently reading. |
| ✅ | #3 | **Of Mice and Men** | John Steinbeck | 1937 | 107 | Already read. |
| ✅ | #16 | **The Call of the Wild** | Jack London | 1903 | 128 | Already read. |

| 1 | #38 | **A River Runs Through It** | Norman Maclean | 1976 | 104 | 🎬 *A River Runs Through It (1992, Robert Redford directs, Brad Pitt stars).* Fly-fishing, family, Montana. Maclean's prose is quiet and beautiful like Johnson's. The river is the landscape and the metaphor. |
| 2 | #18 | **Ethan Frome** | Edith Wharton | 1911 | 105 | 🎬 *Ethan Frome (1993, Liam Neeson).* A frozen New England farm, a trapped man, a devastating sled ride. Wharton writes the cold so well you'll shiver reading it. |

**Cluster vibe:** Beer by a campfire. Dirt under your fingernails. Something sad at the end.

## Cluster 8: Home, Family & Memory

| 7 | #84 | **Mrs. Caliban** | Rachel Ingalls | 1982 | 125 | A lonely housewife falls in love with a six-foot frog-man escaped from a lab. Named the best American novel of 1982. Sounds absurd — and it is — but it's really about real loneliness and real desire. |
| 8 | #55 | **The Fifth Child** | Doris Lessing | 1988 | 133 | A happy family is destroyed by the birth of a violent, possibly inhuman fifth child. A horror novella disguised as domestic fiction. From the Nobel laureate. Deeply unsettling. |
| 9 | #85 | **Bear** | Marian Engel | 1976 | 140 | A librarian on a remote Ontario island enters into a relationship with a bear. Exactly as strange as it sounds. | 10 | #87 | **The Body Artist** | Don DeLillo | 2001 | 124 | A performance artist grieving her husband's suicide encounters a man who replays the past. DeLillo's most experimental work — spare, elliptical, dreamlike. Most challenging read in this cluster. |

## Cluster 10: Sharp Tongues
*Satire, wit, social comedy, and the pleasure of watching a brilliant writer skewer the world. These are the funny ones — or at least the wickedly smart ones.*

**Why save this for last:** After nine clusters of survival, horror, heartbreak, politics, philosophy, crime, family, and aliens, you deserve to end with something that makes you smile. These are also some of the most purely enjoyable reads on the entire list.

| Order | Rank | Title | Author | Year | ~Pages | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | #28 | **The Uncommon Reader** | Alan Bennett | 2007 | 120 | Queen Elizabeth II discovers a mobile library and becomes obsessed with reading, to the horror of her staff. Warm, witty, and a love letter to books. The perfect novella for someone who's just read 90+ of them. |
| 2 | #99 | **The Penelopiad** | Margaret Atwood | 2005 | 199 | Penelope retells the *Odyssey* from the afterlife, exposing what Homer left out. Atwood's voice is sharp and funny. You don't need deep knowledge of the *Odyssey*. |
| 3 | #83 | **Lady Susan** | Jane Austen | 1871 | 88 | 🎬 *Love & Friendship (2016, with Kate Beckinsale — hilarious and highly recommended).* Austen's most deliciously villainous creation: a scheming widow manipulating everyone through letters. Watch the film too. |
| 4 | #76 | **Goodbye, Columbus** | Philip Roth | 1959 | 100 | 🎬 *Goodbye, Columbus (1969, with Richard Benjamin & Ali MacGraw).* A librarian's summer romance with a wealthy girl exposes class tensions. Won the National Book Award. Roth's voice is funny, smart, and cutting. |
| 5 | #27 | **The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie** | Muriel Spark | 1961 | 136 | 🎬 *The Prime of Miss Jean Brodie (1969, Maggie Smith — won the Oscar. One of her greatest roles).* An Edinburgh teacher cultivates devoted students; one will betray her. Spark's prose is precise, glittering, and lethal. Named to both TIME's and Modern Library's 100-best lists. A perfect note to end the entire journey on. |

**Cluster vibe:** A raised eyebrow. A glass of champagne. Laughing at something you probably shouldn't.
